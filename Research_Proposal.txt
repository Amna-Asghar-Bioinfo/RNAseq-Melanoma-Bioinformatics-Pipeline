
Research Proposal
1. Study Title
"Integrative Bioinformatics Pipeline for the Identification of Key Regulatory Pathways and
Potential Therapeutic Targets in Metastatic Melanoma using GSE46517."
2. Biological Problem & Research Background
Metastatic melanoma is the most lethal skin cancer, causing over 72% skin cancer-related deaths
due to its aggressive transition from benign nevi to primary and metastatic stages. By utilizing
differential gene expression (DGE) analysis, we can identify "molecular switches" where up-
regulated oncogenes act as drivers and down-regulated tumor suppressors act as brakes of
disease progression. Mapping these genes to functional networks like the PI3K-Akt or MAPK
pathways reveals the biological systems hijacked by cancer to survive and spread, ultimately
enabling drug repurposing to provide rapid, targeted therapeutic options for advanced-stage
patients.
3. Main Research Question
a. Which genes exhibit significant differential expression (P-value < 0.05, LogFC > 1) in
the GSE46517 metastatic melanoma dataset?
b. Which of these DEGs act as "hub genes" that most likely contribute to disease
mechanisms?
c. Which biological pathways (e.g., Pathways in Cancer, PI3K-Akt) are statistically
associated with these genes?
d. Can these specific genes or pathways be targeted using existing FDA-approved drugs
identified via DGIdb?

5. Methodology
The pipeline was executed through five distinct stages:
1. Data Acquisition: Retrieval of the GSE46517 transcriptomic profile from the NCBI
Gene Expression Omnibus.
2. Statistical Filtering: Genes are classified into up-regulated (LogFC > 1) or down-
regulated (LogFC < -1) categories based on whether their expression levels significantly
increase or decrease in the disease state compared to the control.
3. Functional Annotation (DAVID): Using the DAVID resource to categorize genes into
KEGG pathways.
4. Target Prioritization: Narrowing results to 4 pathways directly involved in oncogenesis
(e.g., Pathways in Cancer).
5. Drug-Gene Interaction (DGIdb): Searching for FDA-approved inhibitors that can "turn
off" the overexpressed genes found in the metastatic samples.

Research Hypothesis & Biological Interpretation
Analysis of the GSE46517 dataset reveals a significant transcriptomic shift where 2,297 DEGs
drive the malignant transition from benign nevi to metastatic melanoma. This progression is
characterized by the robust up-regulation of cell cycle regulators and the downregulation of
extracellular matrix stability genes, effectively hijacking the PI3K-Akt and MAPK signaling
pathways to favor uncontrolled proliferation and metastasis. Cyclinon these results, we
hypothesize that targeted pharmacological intervention using Dinaciclib to inhibit overexpressedcyclin-dependent kinases and Dabrafenib to modulate dysregulated MAPK signaling will
disrupt the metastatic cascade and restore cellular equilibrium. This precision-medicine strategy
is biologically justified by the significant P-values< 0.05 and the clear directional shift in
LogFC values observed in the metastatic samples, confirming these genes as functional
therapeutic targets.
